Developer documentation reborn

I implemented new standards for documentation and built a developer portal to replace low-performing legacy PDFs.

The problem

A technology leader struggled to explain how to build solutions with its products.

  • Wrong format: Information was distributed in dozens of PDFs.
  • Low quality: Instructions were contradictory and unclear. Few code samples existed. There was no starting point or goal.
  • Wasted time: Implementations usually required multiple attempts. Most customers made the same mistakes and required substantial human support.
  • Reputational damage: Customers regularly complained about documentation.
  • No docs team: The company did not have a documentation team.

I was brought in to build the company's first developer portal, write new documentation, and guide its engineering teams to maintain and improve their content.

The solution

We implemented a comprehensive revision for all developer documentation.

  • Docs-as-code: Docs are written in MarkDown, versioned with Git, and tracked with Jira. Software development best practices, like peer review on pull requests, feel natural and improve documentation quality. Developers contribute using tools they already know.
  • CI/CD: GitHub Actions automate builds and deployments.
  • Open source tech stack: Built on Docusaurus and other open source tools, docs are static sites, ensuring speed and reliability while avoiding software costs and vendor lock-in.
  • Linting: Content is linted in real time and on pull requests. This speeds up review, ensures style compliance, and improves writing quality.
  • Data-driven: We can monitor documentation performance with session replays and large-scale behavioral data.
  • Slack integration: Relevant people are alerted when content needs review or a site is deployed.

Read on to learn how we arrived at this solution.

Requirements gathering and planning

Before work could begin, it was important I understand and document the nature of the problem and what kind of solution we needed.

  • Education: I learned what products the company sold and how they worked.
  • Stakeholder research: I met with product managers and customer-facing teams to learn their perspectives on the organization's content challenges.
  • Customer research: I gathered all known customer research and feedback relevant to the project.
  • Competitor analysis: I read competitor documentation and learned how they positioned themselves.
  • Content inventory and audit: I inventoried and audited existing documentation.
  • Report and strategy: I created a report of everything wrong with the documentation and the processes and technology that generated it. Then, I produced a digestible, 5-point strategy so leaders and executives understood what we were doing.

Vendor assessment

The documentation tool we selected would define how documentation was written and distributed for years to come. It was important to fully evaluate options.

  • Market research: I looked at 25 common documentation tools. I inventoried their basic strengths and weaknesses, and noted which were worthy of further evaluation.
  • Shortlist: We shortlisted 3 tools. I joined with one of our dev teams to build proofs of concept and evaluate them more deeply.

In the end, we chose Docusaurus.

  1. Designed for documentation: Documentaton-centric features and strong defaults allowed us start writing quickly.
  2. Well supported: Its community of users and well-written documentation were empowering.
  3. Extensible: Its plugins and extensibile design ensured we could aspire to almost anything.
  4. Compatible: Its React foundation allowed us to integrate it with the company's existing frontends, version control, and cloud services.

This vendor assessment helped us defend our decisions when senior leaders later questioned our reliance on open-source tools. Our validation calmed their fears.

Building

The company struggled to store and deploy documentation assets, so we needed to make our systems both powerful and simple.

  • Design: I worked with a Product Manager, UX designer, and front-end developer to design and implement the site's visual style.
  • Version control: I set up a GitHub repo to version control our development environment. Since GitHub was already widely used, the transition for developers was easier.
  • Deployment: I worked with the DevOps team to automate builds and deployments. We created staging and production environments and relied on GitHub Actions to do the heavy lifting.
  • Authentication: There are distinct advantages to publicly available documentation, but the company was emphatic that its documentation be secured. I had to choose my battles carefully, so I worked with a frontend and backend developer to integrate the site into the main platform's identity service.
  • Monitoring: We implemented the company's tool for event tracking and session replays, allowing us to monitor the documentation's performance over time in high-resolution.

Writing

Fixing the documentation's myriad issues meant rewriting significant amounts of content.

  • Style guide: Consistent style is important, but bespoke style guides are tedious and have low ROI. I accepted the Google Developer Style Guide because it was complete, easy to reference, and appropriate to the company's needs.
  • MVP definition: I worked with the CTO, COO, and VP of Product to define a minimum viable product and delivery deadline. Our initial commitment was at the upper limit of what I was comfortable with, but we all agreed it was possible.
  • Team integration: I introduced myself to the engineering teams I was supporting and walked through the project plan and MVP.
  • Information structure: I mapped the structure and flow of the new documentation. This allowed for rapid rounds of iteration.
  • Task breakdown and estimation: I broke the work into epics and tasks with user stories, acceptance criteria, and time estimations.
  • Peer review: As I completed each component of the documentation, I submitted a pull request to the engineering team. I made adjustments as needed and, once each PR was approved, I merged the docs into the main branch.

Since my colleagues were located on the other side of the world, asynchronous collaboration was key. I was able to move multiple facets of the project forward simultaneously, then allow engineering teams to review PRs and submit feedback overnight.

Enablement

We hadn't just built a new tech stack. We had changed the entire philosophy around what documentation was and how it was to be governed. Education and adoption were the final keys to success.

  • Handbook: I documented the new values, objectives, and methods for writing documentation at the company. This handbook included a 1-week onboarding course, a mix of self-driven learning and classroom time.
  • Onboarding sessions: I met individually with each engineering team, guiding them to take ownership of their own documentation.
  • Content linting: Many of the developers who would contribute to this repo had little experience writing documentation and following style guides. Real-time guidance and automated checks helped them avoid errors.

What went well

  • Perfect execution: We met virtually all initial objectives and timelines, and presented our MVP inside 5 months.
  • Cost: The project had virtually no software or service costs beyond the nominal costs to build and serve the developer portal.
  • Reliability: We experienced no production incidents or downtime.
  • Easy to learn: Developers adoped the system and began producing their own content almost immediately.
  • Source of truth: With a real source of truth, we could begin the sunset process for the collection of legacy sites, systems, and processes the company had relied on to disseminate documentation.

What didn't go well

  • Layoffs: Layoffs and reorganizatons battered the product, engineering, and senior leadership teams. I cycled through multiple managers and project sponsors. Each time, we had to reset expectations and negotiate priorities.
  • Changing goalposts: The initial project sponsors agreed to ship an MVP followed by rapid iterations, but they soon left the company. Later leadership insisted on completing all content migrations before shipping. We missed out on valuable data that could have helped us produce a better result sooner.

Key takeaways

  • "It's getting better, growing stronger": Shipping isn't the end of a project. In many ways, it's the beginning. Session replays, customer feedback, behavioral data, and support metrics will reveal more about how this project has changed the developer experience.
  • "You've gotta fight for your right to party": Organizations do not intrinsically value documentation. In organizations with low content maturity, product designers and content creators must be patient and lobby leadership to think differently about what it means to support customers. Operational maturity is a cultural change that evolves over time.
  • "Don't stop thinkin' about tomorrow": Attrition may sap the strength of technology teams, but focused professionals with a will to succeed can still drive tough projects to completion and deliver value for customers.
