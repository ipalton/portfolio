New Help for HighBond

Seeking to shed our audit-centric reputation and sell more easily to risk management and compliance professionals, we rebranded our GRC platform and supported new types of users with new types of content.

The Problem

The company had a reputation as a strong audit solution. Despite having strong risk management and compliance solutions, the market generally did not recognize us as the comprehensive governance, risk, and compliance (GRC) solution we were.

  • We would rebrand our SaaS platform as HighBond.
  • We would sell different "flavors" of HighBond that were fundamentally similar, but had language and tools tailored to the needs of our different user communities: audit, controls, compliance, risk management, fraud detection, etc.

To support these changes, we needed to reinvent how we provided help content to our users.

  • Our existing GRC platform was documented as a series of disparate help sites, not as a single platform. Users often weren't sure which app solved the problem they had, nor which help site had the answers to their questions. Search was minimally helpful because it was not federated.
  • Only basic usage data was available, making it difficult to identify exactly where people struggled with our content.
  • We did not have enough technical writers to produce or maintain the volume of content we needed to support all these new user types and personas.
  • Our content had to be localized into six languages.

The Solution

The team had completed a major technology modernization recently. This left us with minimal technical debt, but we had yet to unlock the full potential of our tool set. By putting our existing tools to work in a more effective and mature way, we accommodated the coming product changes exactly.

Improved Analytics and Navigation

A solution for telemetry data collection was already in place, but hardly any interactions were tracked. The first step was to unlock latent value in that analytics tool by implementing high-resolution reporting for every event and interaction. At the same time, the product team was working to better instrument the platform itself.

These two data solutions allowed us develop more detailed insight into how the platform and our content was used. By recreating user sessions, we could examine where things went wrong. Common problems we observed:

  • Search queries that returned irrelevant results.
  • Pages with misleading titles or headings.
  • Direct feedback that a piece of content wasn't helpful.
  • Important features were not used because people didn't know about them.

This data confirmed many of the issues we already suspected. Most notably, it confirmed that users often didn't know which help site to go to, and that we needed to combine our multitude of application-based sites into one platform site.

The data also set helpful baselines for what would become our KPIs. We were able to measure how effectively our users found and engaged with content and monitor changes as we made investments over time.

Getting the right content to the right people by single sourcing

To broaden the user base of our platform, we had to appeal to different types of users more directly. This meant selling them only those parts of the platform that were most relevant to their objectives, and using language and terminology that resonated with them.

The implications on content were substantial. Depending on the product package they purchased, our customers would see substantially different terminology and different sets of tools and applications.

It became clear that to properly support these "flavors" of HighBond, we also needed different "flavors" of HighBond help that reflected what users saw in the product. This meant increasing our overall content delivery 600%, and the team was not large enough to accommodate that kind of growth.

Fortunately, the design of the platform and the documentaton was well-suited to single-sourcing. By implementing variables and content conditions, we were able to maintain a single content base as we always had, but use that base to build multiple similar but distinct outputs. Those who bought "AuditBond" saw audit-related terminology and guidance in the documentation, while those who bought "RiskBond" saw risk management terminology and guidance.

Through disciplined use of single-sourcing, we were able to substantially increase the volume and diversity of the content we delivered with only minimal growth in upkeep. Most importantly, our users always saw one documentation site that was an exact match for the product they used, regardless of what it was.

Localization

We had a well-established localization process for our content, but that process would not accommodate variables and conditional content. We worked with the localization team in advance to ensure they could accommodate our vision.

  • We adjusted the localization process. Instead of localizing from the source content, it would localize from the output, after variables and conditions had been accounted for.
  • Thanks to translation memory, most of our existing translations could be reused with small modifications.
  • Because we were standardizing terminology between the product and the documentaton, the l10n team was able to build a consistent translation style guide and controlled terminology, which they applied to all aspects of the platform, the documentation, and other content.
  • We were able to detect a number of bugs in our help authoring tool and translation management system. We worked with our vendors to identify the deficiencies and recommend solutions, which they implemented.

As a result of our collaboration and early planning, we were able to go to market with translations live for our most critical solutions in HighBond. Lower-priority translations were merged in shortly after.

What Didn't Go Well

Few projects go perfectly, and this one was no exception.

  • Since we had switched from localizing source content to output content, ongoing translation costs grew. Similar content had to be localized several times because each instance was slightly different. This increased the amount of translation we needed, and the amount of time the l10n time spent sending and merging new batches of translations.
  • There were problems with the way the company divided and sold the platform. People regularly wanted capabilities from multiple "flavors". Our product and sale model limited our options for up-selling to multiple solutions.
  • Consistent with what we saw in the platform itself, documentation users often searched for content pertaining to capabilities that weren't available in their "flavor" of HighBond. We began including content for features they didn't have, to make it clear the capabilities they wanted existed but they couldn't use them. Over time, our various help sites became nearly identical in scope and structure. This served our users well, but questioned the true value of our single-sourcing model.

Outcome and Legacy

This project had some important takeaways.

  • We increased time on page 13.8% and decreased pages viewed per session 25%. This was an important KPI that signaled users who were finding what they needed easily, and then engaging with it.
  • Searches per session were down 21% while the search result click-through rate was up 23%. This suggested a marked improvement in the search experience.
  • When asked "Was this helpful?", 63% of our readers responded "Yes".
  • Our team's work was regarded as an important part of the platform. A number of our customers expressed gratitude for the strength of our documentation. One of our regional user groups disbanded, citing the quality of our docs as a key motivator. They simply didn't need each others' support anymore.
  • MadCap, one of our vendors, considered our solution innovative enough to publish a case study about us.
  • We were purchased for one billion dollars, largely on the strength of our GRC platform.
