Project Management App

Taskly is a project management app that helps you organize your projects and tasks whatever way makes sense to you. I designed Taskly from scratch, through market research, persona development, requirements gathering, prototyping, and user testing.

1. Competitor Analysis

I evaluated two of the top project management apps on the market (Basecamp and Asana), and a third, less-well-known option (TeamWeek). After using their products and evaluating their websites, marketing, and business, I was able to answer three questions.

  • What is Taskly's industry?
  • Who are my potential customers?
  • What needs do customers have and how can I fulfill those needs?

Competitor analysis of Basecamp, Asana, and TeamWeek.

2. User Research

I needed to understand potential users and their specific needs before I could define a minimum viable product, so I surveyed and interviewed people who indicated they used project management tools. I asked the following questions:

  1. Does existing project management software allow you to control and adjust your priorities in a way that feels natural and useful?
  2. Is current project management software responsive when unexpected priorities arise?
  3. Does current project management software allow you to view projects, tasks, and responsibilities the way you want to?
  4. Does current project management software make it easy for projects or tasks to change size or scope part-way through?
  5. If you could change something about project management software, what would it be?

A survey question demonstrating great disagreement among respondants.

The survey yielded interesting results.

  • More than half of respondents said they could control and adjust priorities in a way that felt natural and useful. This was interesting, as there were actually few prioritization tools available in these apps.
  • Responses were mixed when respondents were asking if project management software was responsive when unexpected priorities arose.
  • Nearly a quarter of users felt that project management software did not allow them to view things the way they wanted to.
  • Responses were mixed, but weighted toward no, when I asked if things could change scope easily.
  • Multiple respondents requested newsfeeds/dashboards to help them grasp their projects and progress.
  • Multiple respondents requested a reduction in complexity, while multiple others requested more customization.

A survey question with polarized results.

3. Personas

With my initial research done, I could begin to create personas and think about what should go into a minimum viable product.

  • I defined three personas. I wanted the design process to constantly remember that we were designing for real people who had real goals and worked in a variety of circumstances and environments.
  • I created task models and customer journey maps.
  • I defined a minimum viable product. I wasn't going to make the mistakes of some startups and overcommit without doing enough research. I needed firm goals on which I could actually deliver, and I needed to be lean so I could pivot if something didn't work.

A persona representing a Taskly user. It focuses on her responsibilities and circumstances.

A task model, highighting the complex user thought processes that may go into a single app flow.

4. Information Architecture

Because designers can't represent their users, I needed to understand the mental models of my audience.

  • I used card-sorting to see how potential users related to concepts like projects, tasks, chat, people, viewing, sorting, etc.
  • I defined a sitemap for the app using the results of the card sorting and other user research.
  • I evaluated the SEO of my competitors and wrote web content that used some of my competitors' top keywords. While this wouldn't strongly influence the app, it would ensure our website was more visible in search results.

A similarity matrix showing the strength of the relationship between each card.

A sitemap showing the basic blueprint for the app.

5. Paper Prototyping

One of the keys to a lean, iterative design process involves minimizing wasted time. I started with quick pen-and-paper thumbnails to visualize my ideas and solve problems early on. Once I was happy with the general idea, I sketched more polished flows from which I could start to build a prototype.

Unpolished thumbnails breathe life into design ideas and start the prototyping process.

A more polished sketch showing the flow for switching to a Calendar view type.

A more polished sketch showing the flow for switching to a Gantt Chart view type.

6. Wireframes and User Testing

As the screens and flows within the app solidified, I created digital wireframes for all my different screen types using UXPin. Then I used InVision to test key workflows with potential users.

The first round of testing didn't expose overt usability problems with the app, but it did reveal that the test might have been invalid. Projects in the list had different shades, which incorrectly emphasized darker elements of the wireframe that were not relevant to the flow being tested, and distracted users from their objectives.

A desktop wireframe showing a list of projects.

A mobile wireframe showing a list of tasks.

The same list of projects in a calendar view.

Removing the shading from the projects list yielded a more effective test. Users were highly successful when given their tasks, but there was one important takeaway. I had to add labels to icons that did not have them, as users distrusted their instincts about icons represented. Combining icons with text is always more information-rich.

7. The Initial High-Fidelity Prototype

With my initial hypotheses confirmed or corrected, it was time to build a high-fidelity prototype. I defined a color-palette, chose appropriate fonts, created a style template, some swatches, a UI kit, and icons. Then, I prototyped in Axure RP 8.

However, I wasn't happy with Taskly. It was okay, but it lacked the modern polish people expected from a UI. The prototype was usable in the sense that people could accomplish tasks, but Taskly wasn't enjoyable to use, and there's more to user experience than usability alone.

My initial UI kit was OK, but it didn't have a level of polish I was happy with.
